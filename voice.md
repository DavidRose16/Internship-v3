# Voice & Style Guide

## Core Voice

Writing should feel sharp, direct, and slightly intellectual without being heavy.

The tone is confident and observant, not performative.
It should sound like someone who understands how things work, not someone trying to prove they do.

Short > long.
Specific > generic.
Clarity > polish.

---

## Rules

- Keep emails under 120 words, ideally closer to 80–100
- No buzzwords, no corporate tone
- No "I am passionate about…" or resume-style language
- No over-explaining or long intros
- Avoid generic praise like "really impressive company"
- Every email must include a real, specific observation

- Do not try to sound overly formal
- Do not try to sound overly casual
- Do not try to impress — assume competence

- Sentences should be clean and controlled, not rambling
- Slight edge is good, but never aggressive

---

## Style Patterns (rotate between these)

### 1. Direct observation (default)
Open with a specific insight about the company.

Example:
"Came across what you're building around [X], interesting how you're approaching [specific angle]."

---

### 2. Problem-first
Start with the underlying problem they're solving.

Example:
"Most teams dealing with [problem] stop at [typical solution], you seem to be pushing further into [specific difference]."

---

### 3. Contrast framing
Highlight what makes them different from others.

Example:
"A lot of companies in [space] optimize for [common thing], this feels more focused on [their actual approach]."

---

### 4. Curiosity-driven
Lead with a genuine question or curiosity.

Example:
"Was looking into how you're handling [specific piece], curious how you think about [nuanced angle]."

---

### 5. Builder-to-builder
Speak from shared experience without overselling it.

Example:
"I've spent time working in similar messy systems, especially around [relevant area], so this stood out."

---

### 6. Slightly contrarian (use sparingly)
Challenge a common assumption in their space.

Example:
"Feels like most of [industry] is solving this in a surface-level way, this seems more grounded."

---

## Structure

- 1–2 lines: observation / hook
- 1–2 lines: light personal connection
- 1 line: direct ask

No more.

---

## Things to Avoid

- Long paragraphs
- Overly structured or "perfect" writing
- Explaining your entire background
- Trying to sound impressive
- Anything that feels like it was written for an application portal

---

## Example Lines

- "Not sure if you're hiring, but figured I'd reach out directly."
- "Spent some time looking into what you're doing — the [specific detail] stood out."
- "I've been thinking a lot about [problem space], especially where things break in practice."
- "Most teams approach this in a surface-level way, this feels more grounded."
- "Happy to help wherever useful."

---

## Example Email

Most tools working with operational data stop at aggregation, you seem to be pushing toward actually making it usable in context.

That stood out, especially given how messy these systems get in practice.

I've spent time building in similar environments where information lives across too many tools and breaks down quickly.

Not sure if you're hiring, but figured I'd reach out directly.
Would be interested in contributing if useful.

Best,
[Your Name]
